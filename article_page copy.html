Overcoming Hurdles for Success

Join me as I recount my personal journey as a freelancer on Upwork, a platform that offers unparalleled freedom and countless opportunities for those pursuing non-traditional career paths. In this article, I will share the ups and downs I encountered and the valuable lessons I learned along the way.

The Challenging Start

Starting on Upwork isn't easy. The first job may not be mind-blowing, but it sets the stage for better opportunities. Writing concise proposals with a brief explanation of why you're the right fit, accompanied by direct links to your best past work, can attract clients' attention. This approach helps drive traffic to your portfolio and showcases your capabilities. When submitting proposals, it's essential to stand out among the competition. Highlight your unique skills and experiences that make you the perfect fit for the job. Craft your proposals carefully, ensuring they are tailored to each specific project and client.

Writing Winning Proposals

Crafting a compelling proposal is crucial to landing a job. Keep it concise, highlighting your relevant experience and skills in just a few sentences. Include direct links to your best work examples, and consider adding more details in your profile's signature. To further enhance your chances of success, take the time to research the client and the project thoroughly. Understand their needs and address them directly in your proposal. Demonstrate your understanding of their requirements and showcase how your skills can provide the solution they are seeking.

The First Contract - An Encouraging Step

My first job on Upwork was a fixed project with a budget of approximately $300. It took me about three days to complete. Being a freelancer from a non-first-world country, this was a reasonable start. However, the initial job is rarely a breeze, and challenges may persist. But don't worry—things do get better.

As a newcomer to Upwork, it's important to view your first job as a learning experience. Focus on delivering high-quality work, exceeding the client's expectations, and building a positive reputation. Completing the first project successfully can open doors to more opportunities and higher-paying jobs.

Importance of Job Success

Successfully completing a job sets you on the right path. Upwork's algorithms recognize your achievements, paving the way for more promising opportunities. While it may be daunting initially, perseverance and dedication pay off in the long run.

Maintaining a high Job Success Score (JSS) is crucial for securing future projects. To improve your JSS, focus on delivering quality work, meeting deadlines, and maintaining clear communication with clients. Actively seek feedback from clients to showcase your professionalism and reliability.

The Rising Talent Badge and Its Caveats

After completing one or a few good jobs, you might earn the Rising Talent badge. However, this badge can make you a target for exploitative clients. Upwork should reconsider its effectiveness, as it exposes talented freelancers to potential risks without commensurate benefits. While the Rising Talent badge may provide some initial recognition, it's important to be cautious. Some clients may take advantage of Rising Talent freelancers by demanding excessive work for low pay or imposing unreasonable expectations. As a freelancer, be discerning when accepting projects and ensure the terms align with your skills and capabilities.

Learning from Missteps

In my second contract, a $30-per-hour project to convert a design into a front-end app, I made a critical error. In an attempt to please the client, I offered a full refund if they were unsatisfied. Unfortunately, this led to unforeseen complications. The client expressed discontent with the project's complexity, and despite my disbelief, I had to refund the money. Though challenging, this experience taught me valuable lessons about setting clear expectations and avoiding unnecessary risks.

It's important to establish clear boundaries and expectations from the beginning of a project. Clearly communicate the scope of work, deliverables, and limitations. Avoid making promises that may compromise your work or financial well-being. Learning from missteps allows you to refine your approach and prevent similar situations in the future.

Dealing with Disputes

In another project, a simple $100 fixed project for app design, a dispute arose when the client requested additional pages beyond our original agreement. I firmly stood my ground, explaining that the scope of work was limited to the initially agreed-upon three pages. To protect yourself, ensure that project descriptions are explicit and leave no room for misinterpretation. Upholding your terms can make a significant difference when disputes arise.

When disputes occur, it's crucial to remain calm and professional. Clearly state your position, referring to the original project description and agreement. Provide evidence, such as written communication and documentation, to support your case. Upwork's dispute resolution process is designed to fairly resolve conflicts, and presenting a strong argument can increase your chances of a favorable outcome.

The Impact of Job Success Score (JSS)

Your Job Success Score (JSS) plays a vital role in your freelance career on Upwork. Working diligently and maintaining a high JSS is essential for securing future projects. However, overworking yourself for extended periods can negatively affect your performance and JSS. It's crucial to strike a balance between work and personal well-being.

To maintain a healthy JSS, it's important to prioritize your well-being and manage your workload effectively. Avoid taking on more projects than you can handle without compromising the quality of your work. Set realistic deadlines and communicate openly with clients about your availability. By maintaining a balanced approach, you can deliver exceptional results while preserving your long-term success.

Link to JSS.

Bouncing Back and Finding Balance

Even after setbacks, it's possible to regain momentum and find success on Upwork. By learning from mistakes and maintaining open communication with clients, you can rebuild your reputation and secure new opportunities. While occasional short-term positions may arise, finding a balance between work and personal life is key to long-term sustainability.

When faced with challenges, embrace them as opportunities for growth and improvement. Use feedback from clients to refine your skills and enhance your offerings. Continuously adapt to industry trends and stay updated with relevant tools and technologies. By demonstrating your ability to learn and evolve, you'll position yourself for success in the competitive freelance landscape.

Upwork has played a pivotal role in my freelance journey, providing a platform for rapid job acquisition and fruitful collaborations. Despite its imperfections, Upwork remains the most flexible platform for both clients and freelancers alike. By navigating the challenges, learning from missteps, and adapting to the evolving demands of the industry, you can thrive as a freelancer on Upwork. Embrace the opportunities, stay resilient, and let your talent shine in the digital marketplace.

If you look at the social media marketing landscape, this shouldn’t come as a huge surprise.

These “best times to post on social media” studies are helpful. But they don’t tell you the best time for you and your audience.

And to get the most eyeballs on your posts, you need to post when your target audience is online and ready to see it.

Why is this so important?

Every social network, including Twitter, Instagram, Clubhouse and Facebook, gives new posts a temporary boost.

And if you time your content to go live when most of your folks are online, you’ll increase the odds that your posts get in front of them.

What’s the best way to figure out the best time for you and your business?

Test and experiment.

For example, over the last few years, we’ve tested over 15 different posting times. And from those experiments, we discovered that Tuesdays at approximately 11am Eastern work best for us.

That’s because we’re a B2B company. So 11am Eastern is a time where most of the United States and Europe is at work. But if you’re B2C you may find that Sunday mornings work best.

Add Context to Your Posts

A common mistake people make with social promotion is just posting a link to their latest piece of content.

Here’s an example of what I’m talking about:

Back in the day, this used to work. But with social media algorithms burying link-based posts, you need to go the extra mile to get seen.

Specifically, you want to add some native content to your social shares.

For example, some time ago I published a report on Google’s organic click-through rate.

And instead of just posting a link on Twitter, I actually outlined some of our most interesting results.

Because I added a little bit of native content to my tweet, it did GREAT. In fact, that Tweet got over 800 retweets, which was a new record for my account.

This works for two reasons:

First, social media algorithms LIKE native content.

In other words, most social media sites aren’t going to promote posts that send people away from the platform. But if you write some great content to go with your link, it can spread.

Second, native content gives people a post that they can share and retweet. That’s because the post itself provides value.

In fact, that’s exactly why most people shared my tweet. The tweet itself had new, interesting content that people wanted to read.

Obviously, Twitter’s character limits mean you can’t add a ton of native content to your post.

But on LinkedIn and Facebook, you can actually write what I call “mini blog posts”.

These mini blog posts are 100-200 words of social media content that provides value… and pushes people to click over to your post.

For example, to promote one of our posts on LinkedIn, I told a little story about how I struggled with digital marketing back in the day.

And this mini post helped that LinkedIn post rack up 39,000 views.

Use Popular Hashtags

Hashtags probably won’t make or break how your social media posts perform. But they definitely can’t hurt. So I recommend testing them out.

The best way to find hashtags to use on your posts is a free tool called hashtagify.

To use it, type in a hashtag that you want to use.

And the tool will let you know how popular that hashtag is… and suggests other hashtags to try out.

Very cool.

Test Paid Promotion

Paid promotion (like Facebook boosted posts and Twitter ads) is a guaranteed way to get more eyeballs on your posts.

That said:

The ROI you get from paid promotion depends a lot on your targeting, ad copy and budget. So it’s not something that I recommend everyone do.

But if you have the budget for it, I do recommend dipping your toes into a paid promo.

This is something we’ve experimented with A LOT here at UpworkFreelance. It’s helped some of our Facebook and Twitter posts get hundreds of clicks.

If you want to boost your posts, here are a few things to keep in mind before you start.

  • Start small and slowly expand: You can run Facebook ads for as little as 10 bucks. If that small test run got results for you, you can expand and sale up.
  • Focus on traffic and clicks: I don’t recommend paying for likes, followers or “engagement”. The point of this whole thing is to promote your content on social media, not to get a bunch of likes.
  • Target small groups: Targeting can make or break how your ad performs. I recommend targeting super small groups that you KNOW will be interested in your post. For example, in our case, we usually target people that have visited our site over the last month (aka retargeting).

Get Influencers to Share

When most people think “promote content on social media”, they think:

“Sharing content on your own account”.

But that’s just scratching the surface. If you have a small following, you actually want to spend your time and effort on getting other people to share your content on social media.

And the best way to do that? Overcoming Hurdles for Success.

With outreach, you get your content directly in front of someone that can share it with hundreds, or even thousands, of followers.

The “catch” is that your outreach needs to be SUPER targeted to work.

For example, a few years ago I was about to publish a new content marketing case study.

And before I published that post, I used BuzzSumo to find people that recently shared stuff about content marketing on Twitter.

I sent each of them a personalized, non-pushy outreach email.

And it worked!

Because I was promoting content that people wanted to read and share, most people were happy to share my post with their audience.

Adapt Posts to Each Social Channel

Snapchat. Instagram. Facebook.

Every social media channel has its own wrinkles.

So it’s important to adapt your posts to fit the platform that you’re sharing on.

For example, I know that most people on Twitter are looking for the latest news in tech, sports and entertainment.

So when I share something on Twitter, I make sure to emphasize that it’s new.

But on Facebook, people are there to see what their friends and family are up to.

So when I write Facebook posts, I tend to get a little bit personal.

Optimize Your Title Tag for Shares

Your title tag isn’t just for SEO.

Why?

Well, when someone shares your content, your title tag becomes the title of that social media post.

This is why you want to use titles that grab people’s attention.

How do you do that?

Write long headlines.

Headlines between 80-100 characters tend to get the most shares.

For example, here’s how you can make your short headlines longer and more compelling.

Short Version: 5 Tips for Content Marketing

Long Version: 5 Powerful Content Marketing Tips (That Work in 2021)

See how that works? You can usually just add a word or two at the beginning or end of your title. And that will boost the odds that people click.

Sometimes we even use a chart or infographic.

This helps our posts stand out in people’s crowded feeds.
